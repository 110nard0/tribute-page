Chinua Achebe

The Man Who Reclaimed a Billion Minds

Chinụalụmọgụ Albert Achebe was a Nigerian novelist, critic, and poet who is hailed as the most dominant figure in modern African literature. His literary debut and magnum opus, Things Fall Apart (1958), is the most studied, taught and read African novel and has been in the global spotlight for decades. Achebe resisted colonial perspectives which dominated the African consciousness, absorbing influences from the intersection of Western and African cultures to create a unique African voice. His writing style incorporated the Igbo oral tradition and combined direct narration with folk stories and proverbs which were an integral part of his childhood experience.

"... a magical writer — one of the greatest of the twentieth century"

Achebe acted as an ambassador for the people of the southern region of Biafra when they attempted to secede from Nigeria in 1967. As the Nigerian Civil War raged on and as starvation and violence took their toll, he played a significant role in liasing with Western nations for support and broadcasting the various war crimes and injustices meted out against the Igbo people by the Nigerian government. After the war ended, he had some involvement in national politics but quickly became disenchanted by the corruption and nepotism he witnessed. He went on to win many important literary prizes and received honorary degrees from more than thirty universities all over the world.

Here is a brief timeline of his life:

  • 1930: Achebe is born on 16th November in Ogidi, in the Southeastern region of Nigeria. His parents were Isaiah Okafo Achebe, a schoolteacher and evangelist, and Janet Anaenechi Iloegbunam, a vegetable farmer and church leader.
  • 1936: Enrolls into St Philips' Central School, in the Akpakaogwe region of Ogidi.
  • 1942: Begins secondary education at Nekede Central School, and passes the entrance examinations for two colleges on graduation.
  • 1948: Gains admission as a Major Scholar in the premier set of the University College, now the University of Ibadan. Receives a scholarship to study medicine, which he forfeits after switching to study English, History, and Theology.
  • 1953: Graduates from university with a second-class degree. Gets an English teacher's job at a secondary school in Oba. Moves to Lagos soon after to start work at the Nigerian Broadcasting Service.
  • 1958: After multiple rejections from publishing houses, he publishes his first novel, "Things Fall Apart", to overwhelming critical acclaim from international critics and the British press. He is promoted at the NBS to head the network coverage of the entire Eastern region. Meets and begins dating his future wife.
  • 1960: Months after Nigeria gains independence, he embarks on a tour of East Africa as a recepient of a six-month travel grant from the Rockefeller Fellowship. Publishes three more books between then and the start of the Biafran War.
  • 1961: Resumes at the NBS as the Director of External Broadcasting where he sets up the Voice of Nigeria Network to present an unbiased perspective of post-independence events. Marries Christie Okoli in the Chapel of Resurrection on the University of Ibadan campus.
  • 1962: Visits the United States and Brazil under a UNESCO Fellowship for Creative Artists. Is appointed the General Editor of the African Writers Series (a major force in exporting literature from Africa to the global community), an office he holds for a decade.
  • 1967: Cofounds Citadel Press to increase the scope of literature available to younger generations. Mid-year, civil war breaks out and he becomes a prime target of the Nigerian government; escapes disaster numerous times - including a bombing of his family house - by relocating to various cities within the eastern region of the country. Writes mostly poetry as a result of living in a warzone.
  • 1969: Joins the National Guidance Committee which drafts a document entitled The Principles of the Biafran Revolution, later released as the Ahiara Declaration. Goes on a group tour of the USA, visiting campuses and conducting a myriad of interviews to raise awareness about the genocide occurring in Biafra.
  • 1970: Relocates with his family to their destroyed home in Ogidi following the surrender of the Biafran army. Takes a job in academia once again, at the University of Nigeria, Nsukka.
  • 1971: Starts two magazines: the literary journal Okike, a platform for African art, fiction, and poetry; and Nsukkascope, an internal publication of the University. Later establishes another cultural magazine, Uwa Ndi Igbo, to showcase the indigenous stories and traditions of the Igbo people .
  • 1972: Moves with his family to the USA to accept a professorship position at the University of Massachusetts, Amherst. Receives the first Commonwealth Poetry Prize.
  • 1976: Returns to the University of Nigeria as a professor of English after his service at UMass Amherst and a visiting professorship at the University of Connecticut. There, he holds a chair until his retirement in 1981.
  • 1983: Becomes active with the People's Redemption Party (PRP) and is appointed the party's deputy national vice-president. Spends the decade making appearances at conferences, delivering speeches and working on novels.
  • 1990: Suffers a severe spinal injury after a motor accident which paralyzes him from the waist down. Accepts a position as the Charles P. Stevenson Professor of Languages and Literature at Bard College in Annandale-on-Hudson, New York and remains there for more than a decade. Spends little time in Nigeria henceforth but continues denouncing the abuse of power by state leaders.
  • 2007: He is awarded the Man Booker International Prize in June 2007. Briefly returns to Nigeria for the International Festival of Igbo culture, to give the Ahajioku Lecture. Publishes The Education of A British-Protected Child, a collection of essays later in the year.
  • 2009: Joins Brown University faculty as the David and Marianna Fisher University Professor of Africana Studies.
  • 2010: Receives one of the richest prizes for the arts, The Dorothy and Lillian Gish Prize for $300,000.
  • 2012: Releases There Was a Country: A Personal History of Biafra, the last publication of his lifetime. It immediately caused a palpable stir among Nigerian citizens and re-opened the discussion about the Civil War.
  • 2013: Dies after a short illness on 21 March 2013 in Boston, United States. The New York Times described him in his obituary as "one of Africa's most widely read novelists and one of the continent's towering men of letters". The BBC wrote that he was "revered throughout the world for his depiction of life in Africa". He was laid to rest in his hometown of Ogidi, Anambra State.

“Chinua Achebe mattered very much to Nigerians in a real way... I think we felt that he gave us our history back. I think we felt there was a dignity he brought back to our story. He’s immortal. The man has died but the spirit lives. I think he’ll be read for a very long time. I think he’s going to mean a lot to Nigerians — to Africans, really — for a very long time.”

— Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie
